Looking into Nathan’s Famous Hot Dog Eating Contest with Closeread

Author

Amara McGowan

Nathan’s Famous Hot Dog Eating Contest is a competitive eating competition where contestants are given 10 minutes to eat as many hotdogs (bun and all) as they can manage. In its early years the duration of the competition varied, but for the past decade it has been a 10-minute cutoff.

If you’re interested in understanding how competitive eaters are able to consume so much food, here is a video referencing Nathan’s Famous Hot Dog Eating Contest and the science behind it.

NoteLearning Objectives

By the end of this tutorial, students will be able to:

  1. Create a “scrollytelling” document with Closeread.

  2. Create scatterplots to look at patterns in data.

  3. Form questions about the data based on visualizations.

  4. Investigate questions about the data.

NoteActivity Length

This could be suitable of an out of class activity or one that spans one or more class periods.

NoteMethods

Technology Requirement:

This activity is designed to be completed primarily in R using a Quarto document workflow. Students should be comfortable importing data and making basic plots with packages from the tidyverse (especially readr and ggplot2).

Data

The hotdog_clean.csv dataset contains 69 rows, one per Nathan’s Famous Hot Dog Eating Contest Winner, with returning winners showing up in the data multiple times. The primary variables in the data are Dogs and Time.

Download Data:

Variable Descriptions
Variable Description
Year Year the Competition took place
Sex Sex of the Winner (0 = Woman, 1 = Man)
Dogs How many hotdogs the winner consumed
Winner First and Last Name of the Winner
Time Duration of the competition in minutes

This module will help you learn how to use the quarto extension closeread to tell stories with your data. To the right is a plot of the amount of hotdogs consumed per minute by winners. When you scroll, you’ll see the plot stays to the right and new text emerges.

This scrolling feature helps you tell stories about your data, some people call it “scrollytelling.”
